About Lok Sabha

As Benjamin Franklin had once said-

"An investment in knowledge pays the best interest."

Well, that is probably the norm we follow in today’s globalised world. Capital inflow across boundaries has changed the way the world economy works and has linked the world as a house of cards where volatility in even the smallest of rooms can lead to the collapse of the whole house. Foreign Direct Investment and Foreign Institution Investors are the two pillars of those capital inflows which have revolutionized the way people invest. The Lok Sabha in the first edition of DPSMUN 2016 has been tasked with playing a decisive role in determining the fate of capital inflows in one of the world’s largest and fastest growing economies as the first agenda of this session of the Lok Sabha deals with FDI and FII on a national scale, with all its positive and negative aspects. Where other economies have a more stable economical framework, India holds a unique position in the centre of world trade and capital flows characterized by its ideal location and prospects of future growth. Even though FDI and FII have been continuously growing and helping in the development of our countries, we shouldn’t turn a blind eye to the other side of the coin which is often neglected in public discussions. This other side represents the fate of those small and marginalized groups who haven’t been able to improve their situations due to monopolistic practices employed by large MNCs.

The second agenda being simulated at the Lok Sabha is the discussion of the Bharatiya Janata Party’s proposed amendments as an Ordinance to the Land Acquisition Bill, which was first introduced as the “The Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act” by the United Progressive Alliance Government in 2013. As per the Act, the union or state governments can acquire lands for its own use, hold and control, including for public sector undertakings and for "public purposes”. The BJP then proposed an Ordinance which altered 9 of the major points of this bill and earned Mr. Modi and his cabinet the title of “anti-farmer” and “anti-poor” and that they were “favoring the industrialists”. This garnered a lot of criticism from the opposition for its lenient attitude towards industrialists, as the proposed amendments remove requirements for approval from farmers to proceed with land acquisition under five broad categories of projects.

Delegates are expected to tackle both of these issues which are extremely pertinent in today’s time with extreme delicacy because while growth is achieved, it should be collective and not discriminative. I shall be looking forward to three days of extremely intense and controversial debate because after all, what is a Lok Sabha without a few flying paper airplanes? Best of luck, delegates!

See you all in July.
